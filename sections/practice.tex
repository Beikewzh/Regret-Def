\section{Perceived Regret in Practice}
\label{sec:practice}

\subsection{What the bound recovers: bandits with UCB}
\label{sec:recover}

In the coverable case the construction is exactly the classical confidence-bound theory under a new name. Let $|\mathcal{S}|=1$, $H=1$ (a bandit) with $N$ arms of mean $\mu_i$, $\mu^{\star}=\max_i\mu_i$, so $f^{\pi}_t=\mu_i$ for ``pull $i$'' and $f^{K_L}_t=\mu^{\star}$. Let $n_i(t)$ be the pull count, $\hat\mu_i$ the empirical mean, and $c_i(t)=\sqrt{2\ln t/n_i(t)}$.

\begin{proposition}[Recovery of UCB and pseudo-regret]
\label{prop:bandit}
The examiner $\Vhi=\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)$, $\Vlow=\hat\mu_{a_t}-c_{a_t}$ satisfies the following. \textnormal{(i)} It is sound with slack $\delta_t\to 0$ with high probability. \textnormal{(ii)} $\rho_t=2c_{a_t}$ is twice the played arm's confidence width, and $\sum_t(f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t)=\sum_t(\mu^{\star}-\mu_{a_t})$ is the classical pseudo-regret. \textnormal{(iii)} $\Rex_T=O(\sqrt{NT\log T})$, matching the UCB1 rate \citep{auer2002ucb}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textnormal{(i)} By Hoeffding, $\Pr(|\hat\mu_i-\mu_i|>c_i)\le 2e^{-2n_i c_i^2}=2t^{-4}$ for each arm at each round; summing over arms and rounds, the bounds $\hat\mu_i+c_i\ge\mu_i$ for all $i$ and $\hat\mu_{a_t}-c_{a_t}\le\mu_{a_t}$ hold for all $t\le T$ with high probability under the standard UCB1 constants \citep{auer2002ucb}. On that event $\Vhi=\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)\ge\mu^{\star}$ (ceiling, $\delta_t=0$) and $\Vlow\le\mu_{a_t}=f^{\pi_t}_t$ (floor). \textnormal{(ii)} Since $a_t=\arg\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)$, the maximizing index is the played arm's, so $\Vhi=\hat\mu_{a_t}+c_{a_t}$ and $\rho_t=\Vhi-\Vlow=2c_{a_t}$; the pseudo-regret identity is immediate from $f^{\pi}_t=\mu_i$ and $f^{K_L}_t=\mu^{\star}$. \textnormal{(iii)} Using $\sum_{k=1}^{n_i}k^{-1/2}\le 2\sqrt{n_i}$ and $\ln t\le\ln T$, $\Rex_T=2\sum_t c_{a_t}\le 2\sqrt{2\ln T}\sum_i\sum_{k=1}^{n_i}k^{-1/2}\le 4\sqrt{2\ln T}\sum_i\sqrt{n_i}\le 4\sqrt{2\ln T}\,\sqrt{N\textstyle\sum_i n_i}=4\sqrt{2NT\ln T}$ by Cauchy--Schwarz, since $\sum_i n_i=T$. \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

Thus $\rho_t$ is not exotic: it is the UCB/LCB pair, and Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} reads off the UCB1 guarantee. When the agent covers its arms, $\delta_t\to 0$ and $\rho_t\to 0$, so the certificate is tight and vanishing, and the agent bounds its distance from $\mu^{\star}$ without ever computing $\mu^{\star}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:bandit}A).

\subsection{The three results, as three pictures}
\label{sec:bandit-pictures}

Figure~\ref{fig:bandit} contrasts the regimes against the best the external view can compute, namely the plug-in estimate $\max_i\hat\mu_i-\hat\mu_{a_t}$ of static regret (and, with a sliding window, of dynamic regret \citep{wei2021master}). With coverage (panel A) examiner regret vanishes with the true regret. \subsection{Use cases and failure modes}
\label{sec:use-fail}

Three concrete uses, all available without ever knowing $V^{*}$. As a \emph{monitor}, $\rho_t$ is an online read of self-assessed room to improve for a deployed agent. As a \emph{comparison metric}, two algorithms on the same stream are ranked by which $\rho_t$ shrinks faster toward its capacity-constrained optimum. As an \emph{audit}, a low $\rho_t$ is trustworthy exactly on the covered region, with Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} marking when no audit is possible and apparent convergence should instead trigger more exploration or external testing.

The same lens makes the two failure modes legible.

\paragraph{(F1) $\rho_t\to 0$ but the learner is not done.}
An examiner that mis-models the world -- for instance, assumes a bandit is stationary when in fact the special arm switches -- collapses $\rho_t$ to zero because its (wrong) inductive bias $\ModE$ rules out the alternative. By Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} this is the bias-gap failure mode: a small $\rho_t$ that is not backed by coverage or by a $\ModE$ that actually contains the truth.

\paragraph{(F2) $\rho_t$ stays large but the learner has the optimal policy.}
An examiner that hedges against alternatives the world does not actually contain -- for instance, assumes non-stationarity in a stationary bandit -- keeps $\rho_t$ above zero even when $f^{\pi_t}_t=f^{K_L}_t$. Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} still gives a valid upper bound, but it is loose; the looseness is the price of a conservative bias.

\label{sec:capacity}

By A3 the learner is representable in $K_L$ bits; by A4 the examiner carries an inductive bias $\ModE$, which is its own form of capacity. Practical instances vary the examiner's capacity while fixing the learner's: action or state aggregation at coarser or finer scales, or linear features of varying rank. The honest read is that the examiner's bias controls how cheaply the ceiling $\Vhi\ge f^{K_L}_t-\delta_t$ can be met. When the examiner is more aggressively aggregated than the learner, the ceiling becomes vacuous and the bridge lemma gives no useful bound (the bias-gap regime of Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree}); when the examiner is finer than the learner, the ceiling can be met but only at additional sample cost, which is the operational meaning of ``the examiner must out-learn the learner.''

\subsection{Building an examiner that out-learns the learner}
\label{sec:building-examiner}

The principle is constructive: $\Vhi$ is any optimistic estimate -- a UCB index \citep{auer2002ucb}, optimistic value iteration \citep{jaksch2010ucrl2}, an ensemble upper quantile \citep{osband2016bootstrapped}, or a posterior upper quantile \citep{osband2013psrl} -- and $\Vlow$ any pessimistic one \citep{kumar2020cql,jin2021pessimism}. The remaining question is how an $\Filt$-measurable examiner can lower-bound $f^{K_L}_t=\sup_{\pi\in\PiK}f^\pi_t$ when the learner realizes only one $\pi_t$. Three mechanisms answer it, none of which is merely a better prior. \emph{Off-policy estimation} of the value of a family of policies the learner never runs, through off-policy temporal-difference learning and general value functions \citep{sutton2011horde,precup2001offpolicy,modayil2014nexting}, is exactly what estimating $\Vhi$ requires. \emph{Asymmetric capacity and a faster timescale} let the examiner track ahead of the policy it grades \citep{pinto2018asymmetric,konda2000actorcritic,borkar1997twotimescale}. And a \emph{calibration target} \citep{foster1998calibration} makes the slack $\delta_t$ shrink on the covered region, rendering ``is the examiner learning?'' operational. By Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} none of the three manufactures information, so the lead is real but capped by coverage; the bound becomes a design rule, namely to separate the critic from the actor and grant it its own budget $K_E$, off-policy targets, and a calibration loss, with the split $K=K_L+K_E$ the open problem the position opens. For a deployed agent that cannot be graded against an optimum, $\rho_t$ is then an online, $\Filt$-measurable read of self-assessed room to improve, and Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} is the operator's warning that a small $\rho_t$ without coverage is unearned.

\subsection{Exploration and intrinsic motivation}
\label{sec:exploration}

One natural coupling treats $\rho_t$ itself as an intrinsic-reward signal: a region where the examiner reports large room-to-improve is, by Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge}, exactly a region where the learner's policy may still be sub-optimal, so directing exploration there has a self-evident incentive. We do not analyse the joint dynamics, and flag it as a limitation in Section~\ref{sec:limits}, but the construction is direct: pass $\rho_t$ to the learner as an exploration bonus, and read the resulting trajectory of $\rho_t$ as the agent's own progress signal.
