
\section{Theory}
\label{app:theory}
\nofreelunch*


\begin{proof}
    We proceed by contradiction and show that, given a history $h$ and an agent policy $\pi$, any examiner that produces a non-vacuous bound will be invalid in some environments. 
    Suppose we have an agent with history $h=\{o_0, ..., a_{t-2}, r_{t-1}, o_{t-1}\}$.
    and that the examiner $\chi$ produces a non-vacuous bound $\rho^\chi(h) < \rho_{bound}(h)$, where we make the perceived regret's dependence on the history explicit. 
    We will show that $V^{\pi} \le V_{unif}$ and $\sup_{\pi'} V^{\pi'} = V^{max}$. This will imply $V^*- V^\pi > \rho^\chi $  (perceived regret does not bound the true regret), establishing the contradiction.

    Given history $h$, we produce an environment $\Env_{bad}$ that generates the desired bounds. 
        
    The agent produces a distribution over its next action given its history. Let $a_{min}$ be the action with minimal probability (this exists since the action space is discrete).
    To define $\Env_{bad}$,
    we let $a_{min}$ have associated reward $\Rew(h, a_{min}) = R_{max}$ and 
    lead to a future where a unique optimal action at each step receives $R_{max}$. On each future step, this optimal action is the one that minimizes the policy's probability. 
    Thus, the optimal policy has value $V_{max}$ as it assigns probability 1 to $a_{min}$,the optimal action, at every step. 
    All other actions lead to 0 rewards forever.
    We also define $\Env_{bad}$ such that its reward  and observation functions are consistent with the history $h$. This is always possible since we put no restrictions on these functions and each element of the history $h$ depends only on the past. 

    Next, we show that $V^\pi(h) \le V_{unif}$ in $\Env_{bad}$. 
    The uniform random policy will have associated value $V_{unif}(h)= \frac{1}{|\Actions|} {R_{max}} + \gamma V_{unif}(h || a_{min}, R_{max},o' )$, where $a || b$ denotes the concatenation of history $a$ followed by $b$. This expressions is due to the fact that one action has reward $R_{max}$ and other actions leads to 0 reward forever.
    Then, consider the value of the policy $\pi$. Policy $\pi$ assigns probability $\pi(a_{min}) \le 1/|\mathcal{A}|$ to the optimal action.
    So, $V^\pi(h) = \pi(a_{min})R_{max} + V^\pi(h||(a_{min}, R_{max}, o') \le V_{unif} $ since $\pi$ assigns less (or equal) probability to each optimal action. 

    Thus, $V^\pi \le V_{unif}$ and $V^* = V_{max}$ as desired.
    
\end{proof}



\section{Exploration via examiner uncertainty}
\label{sec:intrinsic_optimization}


The examiner has so far been a passive scorer, but its uncertainty can also drive the agent. We turn that uncertainty into an intrinsic reward: each pull is rewarded by the information it yields the examiner, measured as the shrinkage of its confidence interval, $r_{IG}(a) = C_k(a) - C_{k+1}(a)$, where $C_k(a) = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln t / k}$ is the examiner's confidence width after $k$ pulls of action $a$ (and $C_0 := \Rmax$). The agent's reward is shaped to $\mathrm{clip}(r + \beta\, r_{IG}(a),\,0,\,1)$, with no other novelty signal. Rewarding an uncertainty estimate to encourage exploration is the basis of count-based and optimism-driven exploration and of intrinsic motivation more broadly \citep{strehl2009pacmdp, auer2008near, oudeyer2007intrinsic, lidayan2025bampf}; here the signal is supplied by the examiner rather than built into the agent, so the same quantity that scores the agent can also drive it.

In a switching world whose action set grows over time, this bonus lowers cumulative true regret far below the no-bonus baseline and re-adapts faster after every change, since the examiner's confidence re-widens on the arms whose value may have shifted and the bonus prompts the agent to re-sample them (Figure~\ref{fig:intrinsic_beta}). The signal is self-limiting: an arm's total information gain telescopes to $C_0 - C_k \le \Rmax$, so the agent's whole intrinsic budget is bounded by $N\,\Rmax$ independent of the horizon, and an arm stops paying once it is well sampled. The improvement is therefore robust to the bonus strength, decreasing monotonically over a wide range of $\beta$; the over-exploration induced by a large $\beta$ is a bounded, front-loaded cost rather than one that grows with $T$, and it appears only at very large $\beta$, where the bonus saturates the shaped reward and the true reward is effectively ignored.

\section{Relative capacity between agent and examiner}
\label{app:capacity}

A further axis, orthogonal to the dynamics mismatch of Section~\ref{sec:practice}, is representational capacity: how finely the examiner, or the agent, resolves the action space. We probe it by letting one party distinguish only a coarse grouping of the arms while the other stays fine-grained. We expect $\rho^\chi_t$ to stay valid and tight only when the examiner is at least as fine-grained as the distinctions that drive regret, and, when the agent is instead the coarser party, to remain valid while exposing the agent's structural limitation.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{image/fig4_capacity_forward.pdf}\\[6pt]
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{image/fig4_capacity_reverse.pdf}
\caption{Relative capacity in the switching bandit, one cell per resolution, labelled by group size (actions per group). \textbf{(Top, forward).} A fine-grained agent assessed by an examiner that resolves only $G$ groups: a coarse examiner is blind to within-group changes and under-reports, with $\rho^\chi_t$ falling below the true regret (red), while only the fully resolved examiner tracks it. \textbf{(Bottom, reverse).} An agent restricted to $G$ groups assessed by a fully resolved examiner: $\rho^\chi_t$ plateaus just above the structural gap (labelled) the restricted agent cannot close, while the fully resolved agent drives both regrets toward zero. Red: true regret; blue: $\rho^\chi_t$.}
\label{fig:capacity}
\end{figure}

\textbf{A coarse examiner under-reports.} When the agent is fine-grained but the examiner aggregates arms (Figure~\ref{fig:capacity}, top), only a sufficiently fine examiner reacts to the within-group changes the agent must track. As the examiner coarsens it grows blind to those changes and its $\rho^\chi_t$ falls below the true regret, an optimistic and invalid assessment that would wrongly certify the agent as near-optimal. This is the failure of the lower-bound and optimism assumptions when the examiner is too coarse for the world.

\textbf{A restricted agent reveals a structural gap.} When the examiner is fine-grained but the agent is restricted (Figure~\ref{fig:capacity}, bottom), the limitation is the agent's. Here $\rho^\chi_t$ stays a valid upper bound and settles just above the residual gap the restricted agent cannot close, a gap that grows as the agent coarsens, while a fully resolved agent drives both regrets toward zero. Perceived regret thus attributes the persistent regret to the agent's capacity rather than masking it.

We repeat the capacity probe in the stationary world, where the effect is milder because there are no changes to expose a coarse examiner. Figure~\ref{fig:capacity_stationary} shows both directions. In the forward case, once the agent has identified the optimal arm the examiner's aggregation barely matters: all resolutions $G$ converge to nearly the same small $\rho^\chi_t$, since the only distinction that drives regret (optimal versus sub-optimal arm) is preserved under every grouping, with the early-transient separation visible in the inset. In the reverse case, a capacity-restricted agent again plateaus at the structural gap induced by its grouping, and the fully resolved examiner's $\rho^\chi_t$ tracks that gap from above. The stationary world therefore exercises the same mechanism as Figure~\ref{fig:capacity} but without the nonstationary stress that makes a coarse examiner visibly invalid.

\section{Experimental details}
\label{app:details}

\paragraph{World.} All bandit experiments use a bounded-Gaussian arm model: a pull of arm $i$ returns $r = \mathrm{clip}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\sigma^2),0,1)$ with noise $\sigma = 0.1$. There are $N = 10$ arms whose means form a fixed ladder of values evenly spaced over $[0.1, 0.9]$, so adjacent arms differ by only $\approx \sigma$ and separating the best arm from its nearest competitors is a genuine statistical problem. The ladder is assigned to the arms by a random permutation, redrawn for every seed so that no two seeds face the same task. The stationary world keeps this assignment fixed for $T = 10{,}000$ steps; the switching world runs five segments of $10{,}000$ steps and re-permutes the ladder at each of the four boundaries, so the entire ranking of the arms changes between segments rather than only the identity of the best arm. All curves report the mean over $100$ seeds with $95\%$ bootstrap intervals, and per-step regret is shown after a moving-average smoothing.
\paragraph{Agents and examiners (Figure~\ref{fig:practice_main}).} Each examiner keeps, for every arm, an empirical mean $\hat\mu_i$ from $n_i$ observed pulls and a Hoeffding half-width $c_i = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$, and reports the optimistic aspiration $\Vhi_t = \max_i(\hat\mu_i + c_i)$ together with the lower confidence value $\Vlow_t = \hat\mu_{a_t} - c_{a_t}$ on the played arm, so that $\rho^\chi_t = \Vhi_t - \Vlow_t$ (clipped to $[0,\Rmax]$ with $\Vhi_t \ge \Vlow_t$). The examiner variants differ only in which observations enter the counts: a full-memory bracket uses all past data, a sliding-window bracket uses the last $W$ steps, and a reset-aware bracket clears its counts at each known boundary. In the stationary world the near-optimal agent is UCB1 \citep{auer2002ucb} and the poorly-learning agent is $\varepsilon$-greedy with $\varepsilon = 0.1$; the good (matched) examiner is the full-memory bracket and the bad (mismatched) examiner is the sliding-window variant that assumes the world keeps changing. In the switching world the roles invert: the near-optimal agent is a reset-UCB that resets at the known boundaries and the poorly-learning agent is UCB1; the good examiner is reset-aware and the bad examiner is the full-memory bracket that assumes the world is static.

\paragraph{Capacity (Figures~\ref{fig:capacity} and \ref{fig:capacity_stationary}).} Capacity is varied by aggregating the arms into $G \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ equal groups, so the coarse party distinguishes only $G$ groups while the other stays per-arm. The agent is a sliding-window UCB and the examiner is reset-aware. In the forward setting the examiner is coarsened and the boundaries are placed so that a low-resolution examiner cannot see them; in the reverse setting the agent is coarsened and the examiner is per-arm.

\paragraph{Learner comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:learner_comparison}).} Five learners are run in a switching world whose arm set grows from $10$ to $30$ across segments, each assessed by the single reset-aware examiner: UCB1 \citep{auer2002ucb}, sliding-window UCB and discounted UCB \citep{garivier2011switching}, EXP3.S \citep{auer2002nonstochastic}, and Thompson sampling \citep{thompson1933likelihood}. The reported rank correlation is Spearman $\rho_S = 0.85$ over the $5\times100$ (learner, seed) pairs.

\paragraph{Intrinsic motivation (Figure~\ref{fig:intrinsic_beta}).} The bonus added to the agent's reward is $\beta$ times the examiner's per-pull information gain $r_{IG}(a) = C_k(a) - C_{k+1}(a)$, where $C_k(a) = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln t_{\mathrm{eff}}/k}$ is the confidence width after $k$ pulls and $C_0 := \Rmax$; the shaped reward is clipped into $[0,1]$ and carries no other novelty term. The world is a growing-action switching bandit (arm set $10 \to 50$) and the examiner is reset-aware. We sweep $\beta \in \{0, 50, 100, 500, 1000\}$, with $\beta = 0$ the no-bonus reference.
