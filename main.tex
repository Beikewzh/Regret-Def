\summary{
Regret is the standard yardstick of learning, but every existing notion is defined against an environment-supplied optimum $V^{*}$ that must be specified externally. Under the big-world hypothesis (where the agent is far smaller than its world and, following the continual-RL view of \citet{abel2023definition}, never stops learning), no such external optimum is available, and standard regret is undefined or uncomputable. We propose \emph{examiner regret}: split the agent into a learner that acts and an examiner that forecasts an upper and a lower bound on policy value, each carrying a capacity budget in bits, and define regret as the gap between the two bounds. We show this gap upper-bounds true dynamic regret when the examiner is calibrated and consistent, that it recovers UCB, PAC-MDP, and Bayes-adaptive regret as instances of one posterior construction, and that in arbitrary big worlds it provably degrades to a diagnostic rather than a certificate. \begin{abstract}
Regret measures learning by comparing an agent to an optimum $V^{*}$ that, in every standard formulation, is supplied from outside the agent through a model, a prior, or a fixed comparator class. The big-world hypothesis underlying continual reinforcement learning denies the agent any such external object: the world is far larger than the agent, and the best agents never stop learning. We propose \emph{examiner regret}, a regret defined entirely from the agent's internal state. We split the agent into a \emph{learner}, which selects actions, and an \emph{examiner}, which forecasts an upper bound and a lower bound on policy value; each module carries a separate capacity budget in bits. Examiner regret is the gap between the two bounds. We prove a master inequality (Theorem~\ref{thm:master}) showing that examiner regret upper-bounds true dynamic regret whenever the examiner is calibrated and consistent; we identify a single canonical examiner, the Bayesian posterior over a model class, of which UCB, PAC-MDP, and Bayes-adaptive regret are computable approximations; and we work the stochastic-bandit case in full, recovering the classical $O(\sqrt{KT\log T})$ rate. Outside structured settings the bound provably collapses, and we argue that in big worlds the binding constraints are the learner's exploration and the examiner's capacity rather than algorithmic cleverness. The contribution is conceptual: a language that makes the acting/judging split explicit and exposes capacity allocation, $K = K_L + K_E$, as foundational for continual RL.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Continual reinforcement learning (CRL) studies agents that learn in open-ended, evolving environments rather than converging to a fixed solution. \citet{abel2023definition} make this precise: CRL is the setting in which \emph{the best agents never stop learning}, in contrast to the classical view in which an agent's goal is to identify, once and for all, a policy maximizing long-run reward. This shift is motivated by the \emph{big-world hypothesis}~\citep{javed2025bigworld,lewandowski2025bigger}: even a stationary world can be effectively non-stationary to an agent whose representational capacity is far smaller than the world's complexity, so the agent must continually adapt.

The principal instrument for certifying that an agent is learning is \emph{regret}, the cumulative gap between the agent's return and that of an optimal comparator. Yet every standard regret notion supplies its optimum from outside the agent. Expected regret $\sum_t (V^{*}-V^{\pi_t})$ requires a Markov decision process (MDP) with a well-defined $V^{*}$. Adversarial and hindsight regret require a comparator class $\Pi$ fixed in advance~\citep{morrill2021hindsight}. Bayesian regret requires a prior over models~\citep{russo2017ids}. Dynamic regret requires a known or estimable variation budget~\citep{wei2021master}. In each case the optimum is an external object the agent is measured against but cannot itself access.

This is exactly what a big world denies. If the world is larger than the agent can represent and the agent never stops learning, then a fixed $V^{*}$ is either undefined (the comparator depends on the unbounded future of the stream) or uncomputable (it requires a model the agent cannot hold). The community's foundational definition of CRL embraces never-stopping learning~\citep{abel2023definition,abel2024dogmas}, but the standard yardstick for it still presumes a stopping point. We see this as a gap: \emph{we lack a notion of regret that an agent embedded in a big world could, even in principle, evaluate about itself.}

\paragraph{Present study.}
We propose measuring learning from the inside. We decompose the agent into two modules with disjoint capacity budgets: a \emph{learner} $\mathcal{L}$ that selects actions, and an \emph{examiner} $\mathcal{E}$ that forecasts an upper bound $\Vhi_t$ and a lower bound $\Vlow_t$ on policy value. \emph{Examiner regret} is $\rho_t := \Vhi_t - \Vlow_t$, the agent's own estimate of the gap between the best it believes achievable and what it believes it currently achieves. Our contributions are: (i) a definition of examiner regret and a master inequality (Theorem~\ref{thm:master}) showing $\rho_t$ upper-bounds true dynamic regret whenever the examiner is calibrated and consistent; (ii) a single canonical examiner, the Bayesian posterior over a model class, of which UCB, PAC-MDP, BAMDP, and capacity-limited Bayesian RL are computable approximations, demonstrated by working the bandit case in full (Section~\ref{sec:bandit}); and (iii) an exact-indistinguishability theorem delimiting when the bound collapses (Theorem~\ref{thm:ignorance}), with the consequence that in big worlds the binding constraints are exploration and examiner \emph{capacity}, exposing the allocation $K = K_L + K_E$ as a foundational question. We make no claim to a new algorithm or a tight bound beyond prior work; the contribution is a language for thinking about evaluation in CRL.

\section{Setting and Definitions}
\label{sec:defs}

\paragraph{Setting.} An agent interacts with an environment over a single, never-resetting stream. We make no MDP, POMDP, or stationarity assumption; this is the big-world regime. Rewards are bounded, $r_t \in [0, \Rmax]$, and we measure value over a look-ahead window of $H$ steps. We seek a \emph{definition} of regret computable from the agent's internal state, together with the conditions under which it certifies learning.

\begin{definition}[Stream and history]
\label{def:stream}
A \emph{stream} is a sequence $\{(o_t, r_t, a_t)\}_{t \ge 0}$ of observations $o_t \in \mathcal{O}$, rewards $r_t \in [0,\Rmax]$, and actions $a_t \in \Acts$. The \emph{history} at time $t$ is $h_t := (o_0, r_0, a_1, \dots, a_t, o_t, r_t)$, and $\Hist_t$ is the set of histories of length $t$. By the Ionescu--Tulcea theorem the conditional kernels extend to a measure $\mu$ on $(\mathcal{O}\times\mathbb{R}\times\Acts)^{\infty}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Policy and value]
\label{def:value}
A \emph{policy} is a map $\pi : \Hist \to \Delta(\Acts)$. Its \emph{value} at $h_t$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:value}
f^{\pi}_t \;:=\; \Ex_{\mu, \pi}\!\left[\, \sum_{s=t+1}^{t+H} r_s \;\middle|\; h_t \right],
\end{equation}
the expectation taken over the environment's randomness and the policy's internal randomness, conditional on $h_t$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Reference class and reference optimum]
\label{def:ref}
A \emph{reference class} $\Refclass \subseteq \{\pi : \Hist \to \Delta(\Acts)\}$ fixes the comparator. The \emph{reference optimum} is $f^{*}_t := \sup_{\pi \in \Refclass} f^{\pi}_t$.
\end{definition}

The choice of $\Refclass$ \emph{is} the choice of what counts as learning: constant-action policies (bandit regret), stationary policies (MDP regret), policies expressible in $K_L$ bits (capacity-bounded regret), or behavioural deviations of the current policy (hindsight rationality~\citep{morrill2021hindsight}).

\begin{definition}[Learner--examiner agent]
\label{def:agent}
An \emph{agent} is a pair $\mathcal{A} = (\mathcal{L}, \mathcal{E})$. The \emph{learner} $\mathcal{L} : \Hist_t \to \Delta(\Acts)$ emits the policy $\pi_t$ and is parametrized in $\Theta_L$ with $|\Theta_L| \le 2^{K_L}$. The \emph{examiner} $\mathcal{E} = (U, L)$, with $U, L : \Hist_t \to \mathbb{R}$, emits the ceiling $\Vhi_t := U(h_t)$ and floor $\Vlow_t := L(h_t)$, and is parametrized in $\Theta_E$ with $|\Theta_E| \le 2^{K_E}$. The \emph{total capacity} is $K := K_L + K_E$ bits. The two modules have disjoint codomains: the learner emits actions, the examiner emits value bounds.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Examiner regret]
\label{def:exreg}
$\rho_t := \Vhi_t - \Vlow_t$, with cumulative form $\Rex_T := \sum_{t=1}^{T}\rho_t$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Calibrated and consistent examiner]
\label{def:calib}
The examiner is \emph{$(\delta_t,\eta)$-calibrated} on $[1,T]$ if, with probability at least $1-\eta$, for all $t \le T$,
\begin{align}
\text{(C1)}\quad & \Vlow_t \le f^{\pi_t}_t, &
\text{(C2)}\quad & \Vhi_t \ge f^{*}_t - \delta_t, \quad \delta_t \ge 0, \label{eq:calib}
\end{align}
and \emph{consistent} if, in addition, (C3) $\Gamma_T := \sum_{t=1}^{T}\delta_t = o(T)$.
\end{definition}

Condition (C3) is essential. Without it, the trivial examiner $\Vhi_t = \Rmax H$, $\Vlow_t = 0$ satisfies (C1)--(C2) yet carries no information; its slack is $\delta_t = \Theta(1)$, so $\Gamma_T = \Theta(T)$ and (C3) excludes it. Requiring (C3) forces $\rho_t$ to track genuine learning rather than constant ignorance.

\paragraph{The canonical examiner.}
Definitions~\ref{def:agent}--\ref{def:calib} say \emph{what} an examiner must satisfy, not \emph{how} to build one. There is one canonical construction. Let $\nu_t \in \Delta(\Models)$ be a posterior over a model class $\Models$, updated from $h_t$, and set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:canonical}
\Vhi_t = \Ex_{M \sim \nu_t}\!\big[V^{*}_M\big], \qquad \Vlow_t = \Ex_{M \sim \nu_t}\!\big[V^{\pi_t}_M\big].
\end{equation}
Here $V^{\pi}_M$ is the $H$-step value of $\pi$ under model $M$, that is, \eqref{eq:value} with $\mu$ replaced by $M$, and $V^{*}_M := \sup_{\pi \in \Refclass} V^{\pi}_M$; the true value of Definition~\ref{def:value} is the special case $f^{\pi}_t = V^{\pi}_{\mu}(h_t)$ at the unknown true model $\mu$, so the canonical examiner replaces $\mu$ by the posterior $\nu_t$. Consequently $\rho_t = \Ex_{M\sim\nu_t}[V^{*}_M - V^{\pi_t}_M]$ is the posterior value gap, and every computable examiner below approximates \eqref{eq:canonical} under a particular $\Models$ and a tractable surrogate for $\nu_t$. The framework is therefore not a catalogue of unrelated estimators: it is one object with many approximations.

\section{The Master Inequality}
\label{sec:master}

\begin{theorem}[Examiner regret decomposition]
\label{thm:master}
Let $\mathcal{A} = (\mathcal{L},\mathcal{E})$ act on a stream with reference class $\Refclass$, and let $\mathcal{E}$ be $(\delta_t,\eta)$-calibrated on $[1,T]$. Then with probability at least $1-2\eta$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master}
\DynReg_T \;:=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(f^{*}_t - f^{\pi_t}_t\big) \;\le\; \Rex_T + \Gamma_T.
\end{equation}
If $\mathcal{E}$ is also consistent (C3), then $\DynReg_T \le \Rex_T + o(T)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Step 1 (identity).} For each $t$, insert and cancel the two examiner outputs:
\begin{equation*}
f^{*}_t - f^{\pi_t}_t = \underbrace{\big(f^{*}_t - \Vhi_t\big)}_{\text{(i)}} + \underbrace{\big(\Vhi_t - \Vlow_t\big)}_{\text{(ii)}} + \underbrace{\big(\Vlow_t - f^{\pi_t}_t\big)}_{\text{(iii)}}.
\end{equation*}
This is an algebraic identity; the inserted $\Vhi_t$ and $\Vlow_t$ cancel.

\emph{Step 2 (bound each term).} On the event that (C1) and (C2) hold: term (i) $\le \delta_t$ by (C2); term (ii) $= \rho_t$ by Definition~\ref{def:exreg}; term (iii) $\le 0$ by (C1). Hence $f^{*}_t - f^{\pi_t}_t \le \delta_t + \rho_t$.

\emph{Step 3 (sum, union bound).} Summing over $t = 1,\dots,T$ gives $\DynReg_T \le \Gamma_T + \Rex_T$ on the joint event. By Definition~\ref{def:calib} the joint event of (C1) and (C2) holds with probability at least $1-2\eta$ (union bound over the two events). The consistency corollary substitutes $\Gamma_T = o(T)$.
\end{proof}

The inequality separates two contributions: the learner drives $\Rex_T$ down by acting better, and the examiner drives $\Gamma_T$ down by judging more accurately. Both modules must learn, which is precisely the never-stopping picture of \citet{abel2023definition}, now made into two coupled never-stopping problems. The identity also abstracts a step common to optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty analyses: in UCRL2 and UCBVI the per-episode regret is controlled by the gap between an optimistic value and the realized value~\citep{jaksch2010ucrl2}, which is exactly $\Vhi_t - \Vlow_t$ once the optimistic and pessimistic estimates are named as examiner outputs. What is elementary here is the bookkeeping; what is not is the question of which examiners can keep $\Gamma_T$ sublinear, addressed next and in Section~\ref{sec:limits}.

\paragraph{Definition sanity checks.}
\emph{Operational meaning:} $\rho_t$ is the agent's self-estimated reward gap, in reward units, between the best policy it can recognize and the policy it runs. \emph{Reward-shift invariance:} under $r \mapsto r+c$ both $\Vhi_t$ and $\Vlow_t$ shift by $cH$, so $\rho_t$ is unchanged; under $r \mapsto \lambda r$, $\rho_t \mapsto \lambda \rho_t$, scaling linearly as regret should. \emph{Special case:} for $|\States|=1$, $H=1$, the definition reduces to bandit regret (Section~\ref{sec:bandit}). \emph{Non-vacuity:} (C3) excludes the trivial examiner, and $\rho_t > 0$ exactly when the agent believes it can still improve. One well-posedness caveat: $f^{*}_t$ need not be attained for infinite $\Refclass$; Theorem~\ref{thm:master} uses only $f^{*}_t \le \Vhi_t + \delta_t$, so the upper bound is unaffected, while the lower-bound program of Section~\ref{sec:limits} uses a compact $\Refclass$ or an $\epsilon$-net.

\section{Worked Case: the Stochastic Bandit}
\label{sec:bandit}

We work the simplest case in full to show the framework recovers a classical bound and is not vacuous.

\paragraph{Setting.} $K$ arms, $a \in [K]$, reward $r \in \{0,1\}$ with $\Pr(r=1\mid a) = \mu_a$; one state, $H=1$. Let $\Refclass = \{\pi^{(a)}: a \in [K]\}$ be the constant-arm policies, so $f^{*}_t = \mu^{*} := \max_a \mu_a$ and $f^{\pi_t}_t = \mu_{a_t}$. Write $N_a(t)$ and $\hat\mu_a(t)$ for the pull count and empirical mean after $t$ rounds.

\paragraph{Examiner and learner.} With $c_a(t) := \sqrt{2\log t / N_a(t-1)}$, set
\begin{equation*}
U(h_t) = \max_a\big[\hat\mu_a(t-1) + c_a(t)\big], \qquad L(h_t) = \hat\mu_{a_t}(t-1) - c_{a_t}(t),
\end{equation*}
and let the learner be UCB1, $a_t = \arg\max_a [\hat\mu_a(t-1) + c_a(t)]$.

\begin{proposition}[Calibration of the bandit examiner]
\label{prop:bandit}
The examiner above satisfies (C1) and (C2) with $\delta_t = 0$, each with per-round failure probability at most $2t^{-3}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix $t$ and arm $a$. Conditioned on $N_a(t-1)=n$, the rewards of arm $a$ are i.i.d.\ in $[0,1]$ with mean $\mu_a$. Hoeffding's inequality gives
\begin{equation*}
\Pr\!\big(|\hat\mu_a(t-1) - \mu_a| \ge c_a(t) \mid N_a(t-1)=n\big) \le 2\exp(-2 n\, c_a(t)^2) = 2\exp(-4\log t) = 2t^{-4}.
\end{equation*}
A union bound over $a \in [K]$ (with $K \le t$ after the first $K$ rounds) gives, with probability at least $1-2t^{-3}$, simultaneously for all $a$: $\mu_a \le \hat\mu_a(t-1) + c_a(t)$ and $\hat\mu_a(t-1) - c_a(t) \le \mu_a$. Taking the maximizing arm in the first inequality yields $\mu^{*} \le \max_a[\hat\mu_a(t-1)+c_a(t)] = \Vhi_t$, i.e.\ (C2) with $\delta_t=0$. Taking $a=a_t$ in the second yields $\Vlow_t \le \mu_{a_t} = f^{\pi_t}_t$, i.e.\ (C1).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Recovery of UCB1]
\label{cor:ucb}
$\Ex[\Rex_T] = O(\sqrt{KT\log T})$, hence by Theorem~\ref{thm:master}, $\Ex[\DynReg_T] = O(\sqrt{KT\log T})$, recovering the bound of \citet{auer2002ucb}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:bandit}, $\delta_t=0$ and $\Gamma_T=0$, so Theorem~\ref{thm:master} gives $\DynReg_T \le \Rex_T$ on an event of probability at least $1 - \sum_t 2t^{-3} = 1 - O(1)$. Because the learner is UCB1, the chosen arm is the maximizer of the upper index, so $\Vhi_t = \hat\mu_{a_t}(t-1) + c_{a_t}(t)$, and
\begin{equation*}
\rho_t = \big[\hat\mu_{a_t}(t-1)+c_{a_t}(t)\big] - \big[\hat\mu_{a_t}(t-1) - c_{a_t}(t)\big] = 2c_{a_t}(t) = 2\sqrt{\tfrac{2\log t}{N_{a_t}(t-1)}}.
\end{equation*}
Summing and grouping by arm, then using $\sum_{n=1}^{N}1/\sqrt{n} \le 2\sqrt{N}$ and Cauchy--Schwarz ($\sum_a \sqrt{N_a(T)} \le \sqrt{K\sum_a N_a(T)} = \sqrt{KT}$),
\begin{equation*}
\Rex_T \le 2\sqrt{2\log T}\sum_{a=1}^{K}\sum_{n=1}^{N_a(T)}\frac{1}{\sqrt n} \le 4\sqrt{2\log T}\sum_{a=1}^{K}\sqrt{N_a(T)} \le 4\sqrt{2}\,\sqrt{KT\log T}. \qedhere
\end{equation*}
\end{proof}

Examiner regret here is literally twice the UCB confidence width, and Theorem~\ref{thm:master} reproduces the classical analysis. Our contribution is to name this object and detach it from the specific algorithm: the same recipe (choose $\Models$, derive $(U,L)$, verify (C1)--(C3) by concentration) applies whenever such bounds exist.

\section{When the Bound Collapses, and Why It Matters}
\label{sec:limits}

The framework is conditional, and we state its scope honestly. There are three regimes. In the \emph{tight} regime (stationary or structured), (C1)--(C3) hold with $\delta_t \to 0$ via Hoeffding, PAC-MDP, or posterior contraction, and Theorem~\ref{thm:master} matches known rates. In the \emph{loose} regime (bounded non-stationarity), (C1)--(C2) hold with $\delta_t$ controlled by a variation budget~\citep{wei2021master}, giving a valid but loose bound. In the \emph{diagnostic} regime (arbitrary big world), no examiner can guarantee a small slack against a rich $\Refclass$ that the learner does not explore; Theorem~\ref{thm:master} still holds but is uninformative, and $\rho_t$ becomes a diagnostic of the agent's self-perceived gap rather than a certificate. This last regime is not a defect: it is exactly what the big-world hypothesis predicts. The next result makes the obstruction precise and shows it is information-theoretic, not a matter of algorithmic skill.

\begin{theorem}[Contented ignorance]
\label{thm:ignorance}
Fix $\epsilon \in (0, \tfrac12)$ and an integer $N \ge 2$. There is a family of $N$ bandit environments $\{\mu_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ and a learner $\pi$ such that the history $h_t$ has the same law under every $\mu_i$, and any examiner that is a measurable function of the history and satisfies the calibration conditions \textnormal{(C1)--(C2)} on the whole family with slack $\delta_t$ obeys, on every $\mu_i$ and for all $t$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ignorance}
\rho_t \;\ge\; \epsilon - \delta_t .
\end{equation}
In particular, on the environment $\mu_1$ in which $\pi$ is optimal (true regret $0$), a consistent examiner ($\delta_t \to 0$) reports $\rho_t \ge \epsilon - o(1) > 0$ indefinitely; conversely, any examiner that drives $\rho_t \to 0$ violates \textnormal{(C2)} on $\mu_i$ for some $i \ge 2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Construction.} Take $K \ge N$ arms with Bernoulli rewards. In $\mu_1$ every arm has mean $\tfrac12$, so arm $1$ is optimal and $f^{*}_{\mu_1} = \tfrac12$. For $i \ge 2$, let $\mu_i$ agree with $\mu_1$ except that arm $i$ has mean $\tfrac12 + \epsilon$, so arm $i$ is optimal and $f^{*}_{\mu_i} = \tfrac12 + \epsilon$. Let $\pi$ pull arm $1$ at every step; then $f^{\pi}_{\mu_i,t} = \tfrac12$ for all $i$.

\emph{Step 1 (identical histories).} Under $\pi$ only arm $1$ is observed, and its reward is $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\tfrac12)$ in every $\mu_i$ by construction. Hence the law of $h_t$ is identical across all $\mu_i$. Realizing all environments on a common probability space through this shared history, the examiner's output $(\Vhi_t, \Vlow_t)$, a fixed measurable function of $h_t$, is the \emph{same random variable} under every $\mu_i$.

\emph{Step 2 (floor).} Since $f^{\pi}_{\mu_i,t} = \tfrac12$, condition (C1) gives $\Vlow_t \le \tfrac12$ on the calibration event.

\emph{Step 3 (ceiling).} Because $\Vhi_t$ does not depend on $i$ while (C2) must hold for each $\mu_i$, on the calibration event
\[
\Vhi_t \;\ge\; \max_{1 \le i \le N}\big(f^{*}_{\mu_i} - \delta_t\big) \;=\; \tfrac12 + \epsilon - \delta_t .
\]

\emph{Step 4 (gap).} Combining Steps 2 and 3, on the calibration event and for every $\mu_i$,
\[
\rho_t = \Vhi_t - \Vlow_t \;\ge\; \big(\tfrac12 + \epsilon - \delta_t\big) - \tfrac12 \;=\; \epsilon - \delta_t ,
\]
which is \eqref{eq:ignorance}. If $\delta_t \to 0$ then $\rho_t \ge \epsilon - o(1)$ on $\mu_1$, where the true regret of $\pi$ is $0$. If instead $\rho_t \to 0$, then $\Vhi_t \le \Vlow_t + o(1) \le \tfrac12 + o(1)$, which for large $t$ is below $f^{*}_{\mu_i} - \delta_t = \tfrac12 + \epsilon - o(1)$ for every $i \ge 2$, contradicting (C2). \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

The obstruction is not the learner's algorithm but the absence of evidence: because $\pi$ never pulls the alternative arms, no history-based examiner can tell $\mu_1$ from $\mu_i$, so a single calibrated ceiling must hedge against the best hidden alternative and $\rho_t$ cannot fall below $\epsilon - \delta_t$. Escaping the trap requires the \emph{learner} to explore the alternatives, so that histories differ across environments, and the \emph{examiner} to have enough capacity to represent them. This is exactly where the allocation $K = K_L + K_E$ binds, and it is the precise sense in which, in a big world, examiner regret is limited by exploration and capacity rather than by algorithmic cleverness.

\paragraph{The capacity-allocation question.}
With total budget $K = K_L + K_E$, giving more bits to the learner widens the policy class but degrades certification; giving more to the examiner tightens the bound but restricts what the learner can express. We conjecture that the optimal split $K_E^{*}$ depends on environment complexity and is generally interior. Characterizing $K_E^{*}$ for structured big worlds is, in our view, the core theoretical problem this framework opens, and it is distinctively a \emph{continual} question: in a fixed task one can afford a one-time examiner, but under never-ending non-stationarity the examiner must keep paying for itself.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Foundations of CRL.} \citet{abel2023definition} define CRL via agents that never stop learning; \citet{abel2024dogmas,abel2025rethinking} develop the surrounding foundations. Our examiner regret is a candidate yardstick aligned with that view: internal, never-terminating, and capacity-aware. \textbf{Big-world hypothesis.} \citet{javed2025bigworld} and \citet{lewandowski2025bigger} argue the agent is far smaller than its world; our capacity split formalizes one consequence for evaluation. \textbf{Bayes-adaptive and universal RL.} BAMDP~\citep{guez2012bamcp,lidayan2025bampf} supplies the canonical examiner~\eqref{eq:canonical}; AIXI~\citep{hutter2005aixi} is its uncomputable limit. \textbf{Capacity-bounded RL.} \citet{lu2021bitbybit} cast capacity in bits; \citet{arumugam2024capacity} build rate-distortion-projected posteriors, our canonical $K_E$-bounded examiner. \textbf{Non-stationary regret.} \citet{wei2021master} give an optimal black-box dynamic-regret reduction. \textbf{Calibration and PAC.} \citet{foster1998calibration} establish online calibration; KWIK~\citep{li2011kwik} formalizes the ``knows what it knows'' property behind examiner surprise-gating; \citet{strehl2009pacmdp} provide value bounds meeting (C1)--(C2). \textbf{Hindsight rationality.} \citet{morrill2021hindsight} measure regret against a deviation class; our $\Refclass$ generalizes their comparator and makes the certifier explicit. Across all of these the evaluator is implicit or fused with the policy; our distinction is to make the examiner a first-class, capacity-bounded module.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our contribution is a language. Learner and examiner play different roles, pay separate capacity costs, and learn in different ways; making the split explicit recovers existing regret notions as instances of one posterior decomposition (Table~\ref{tab:reductions}), exposes the capacity-allocation question $K=K_L+K_E$, and yields a diagnostic $\rho_t$ usable even where formal bounds collapse. The framework opens a concrete program: characterize $K_E^{*}$ for structured big worlds; design examiners for settings lacking one; complete the stochastic contented-ignorance bound; and test empirically whether separating actor and critic capacity, rather than sharing it, improves continual-RL benchmarks at fixed total parameter count. We claim no new algorithm and no tight bound beyond prior work; the value is in the questions the decomposition makes askable, and in giving continual RL a yardstick that, like its agents, never presumes a stopping point.
