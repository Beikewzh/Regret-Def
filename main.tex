The agent chooses actions to accumulate as much reward as possible and improve its future reward.

A questions arises: how can we measure the performance of a continual reinforcement learning agent from its stream of experience? 


\paragraph{Learning in Any World.} Our setting considers an agent interacting with a general environment. The agent takes actions $a_t$ based on its history and the environment produces rewards and observations $(r_t, o_t)$ based on the entire history.  
No assumptions are placed on the form of the environment; it is not necessarily a MDP, POMDP, etc. Thus, we allow the agent to interact with an arbitrary stream of experience $\{o_0, r_0, a_1, o_1, r_1, a_2, o_2, r_2,...\}$ with the goal of developing an evaluation procedure for intelligent agents that can 
``continue to adapt over the course of lifelong streams of experience." \citep{silver2025welcome}.


\paragraph{Motivation: Standard measures of regret are not suitable for arbitrary streams of experience.} 
Regret is traditionally defined as $V^{*} - V^{\pi_t}$ at timestep $t$ where $V^*$ represents an ``optimal" value and $V^{\pi_t}$ represents the value of the agent's current policy.
There are two classic families of regret definitions which define $V^*$ differently: 1. \textit{Expected regret}, used in stochastic bandits and MDPs. 2. \textit{Hindsight regret}, used in adversarial bandits and online learning. 

For \textit{expected regret}, $V^*$ is the value of the optimal policy, which achieves the largest expected return in the future.
This is is an unreasonable standard for the agent to achieve in a nonstationary environment since this optimal value will depend on quantities inaccessible to the agent, such as how the environment will evolve in the future. 

For \textit{hindsight regret}, $V^*$ is defined as a counterfactual: 
What if the agent had chosen past actions according to a different policy $\pi$ instead? 
Concretely, $V^*$ is the sum of rewards that \textit{would have been accumulated} by the best policy from a given set $\Pi$ if it had been run in the past. 
For example, in classic adversarial bandits, $\Pi$ is the set of policies that always choose a single action.
Then, to achieve low hindsight regret (usually termed ``external regret"), the agent has to do (almost) as well as having chosen the best fixed action in hindsight.
In nonstationary settings or MDPs, it is more difficult to define $\Pi$ without making low regret impossible to achieve \wes{add cite}.

Some works propose other notions of regret to allow for nonstationarity in the problem settings. These include adaptive regret, tracking regret, dynamic regret among others \wes{add cites}.
At a high-level, all these regret definitions limit the amount of nonstationarity to make the problem tractable, with the exact restrictions varying from one to the other. 
For this reason, these regret definitions are unsuitable for our purpose because they explicitly or implicitly place constraints on the environment. We aim to define a performance measure suitable for \textit{any} world.

We define a novel performance measure: The \textit{perceived regret} $\rho^\chi_t$.

\section{Formal Definitions and Assumptions}

We first define the environment and how an agent interacts with it. The environment is framed to allow maximal flexibility and impose minimal constraints on an agent's associated stream of experience. 

\wes{reorganize possibly}

\begin{definition}[Environment] The \textbf{environment} $\Env$ is defined by a tuple $(\Obs, \Actions, \Rew, \Tr, \gamma)$. The observation space $\Obs$ and action space $\Actions$ serve as the agent-environment interface. We let $\mathcal{H}$ be the space of histories, sequences $h_t =\{o_0, a_0, r_1 , o_1, ..., a_{t-2}, r_{t-1},o_{t-1} \}$ for any $t \in \mathbb{N}$. Next, $\Rew:  \Hist \times \Actions \rightarrow \Reals$ is the \textit{reward function} and $\gamma : \Hist \times \Actions$ is the \textit{discount function}, which together define the objective to maximize. Finally, $\Tr: \Hist \times \Actions \rightarrow \Obs$ defines the \textit{observation function}. \wes{maybe add something about observations}
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Agent and Policy]
    The \textbf{agent} $\Lambda$ chooses actions according to a \textbf{policy} $\pi$, defined as a mapping from histories to actions $\pi : \Hist \rightarrow \Actions$.  
\end{definition}
With this definition of a policy, it encompasses any algorithm that modifies the decision-making process based on past data, including memory-based policies or even parameter updates. 

The agent and environment interact in sequence, alternating between the environment producing an observation/reward and the agent choosing an action. These two steps can depend on the entire history of observations, rewards and actions up to that point in time. 


These definitions aims to capture complex streams of experience. In particular, we allow the rewards $\Rew$ and transitions $\Tr$ to depend on the entire history in an arbitrary fashion. In this way, it encompasses a large class of environments, including non-Markovian transition dynamics of any order, adaptive (or adversarial) environments and nonstationarity. 
In particular, we can model ``big worlds" \wes{add cite}, where the agent's computational capacity is tiny compared to the complexity of the world. From the perspective of the agent, the world may appear highly nonstationary and the agent cannot hope to accurately model its full complexity. 


This environment definition has certain limitations as it does not encompass continuous time environments or variable number of observations between actions. We can easily extend our proposed regret definitions to these settings by modifying the definitions of the relevant values but we focus on this setup for simplicity.
We can also augment $\Rew$, $\Tr$ and $\pi$ with an auxiliary argument $\xi \in \Omega$, which allows the rewards, observations and actions to be stochastic by treating $\xi$ as a source of randomness. 

We first define the true value and regret for an agent at a given time in a world. These quantities are unknowable to the agent but are useful to develop the theory. 


\begin{definition}[True Value and Optimal Value]
\wes{maybe call this ``environment value"}
The \textbf{true value} at time $t$ of policy $\pi$ is defined as $V_t^\pi = \Ex_{\substack{o_i \sim \Tr, r_i \sim \Rew, a_i \sim \pi \\ \text{ for } i \in \{1, 2, ...\} }}[\sum_{i=0}^\infty \gamma^i \Rew(H_{t+i}, A_{t+i}) | H_t=h]$ which is the expectation over the future according to the environment's dynamics and rewards, given the past history $h$. 
Then, the \textbf{optimal value} is defined as $V^*_t =\sup_\pi V^\pi_t$
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[True Regret]
    The \textbf{true regret} is defined as the difference between the true optimal value and the policy value. For a policy $\pi$, the regret is defined as $\rho^*_t = V^* - V^\pi_t$
\end{definition}

\subsection{Defining Perceived Regret}


With the basic setting outlined, we can now define our proposed notion of regret. 
We consider an \textit{examiner} $\chi$ that views the same data stream as the agent at every step. 

\begin{definition}[Perceived Regret \wes{add color? highlight somehow. Make text box?}]
The \textit{examiner} $\chi$ maintains estimates of two quantities: an \emph{aspiration} $\Vhi_t$ (``the highest value it thinks is achievable'') and a \emph{lower confidence value} $\Vlow_t$ (``the value it is confident the learner achieves''). We define the \textbf{perceived regret} as $\rho^\chi_t := \Vhi_t - \Vlow_t.$ 
\end{definition}

The following assumptions make this quantity non-trivial:

\begin{assumption}[Valid lower bound]\label{a:lb}
$\Vlow_t \leq V^{\pi_t}_t$ for all $t$. The lower confidence value truly is a lower bound on the policy value. 
\end{assumption}


\begin{assumption}[Optimism]\label{a:optimistic}
$\tilde{V}^*_t \geq V^{*}_t$ for all $t$. The agent overestimates the optimal value in the environment. We can relax this condition to give different guarantees on the performance of the agent. 
\end{assumption}


\begin{proposition}[Validity]\label{prop:valid}  Under these two assumptions, $\rho^\chi_t$ upper bounds the true regret: 
\[
\rho^\chi_t = \underbrace{(\Vhi_t - V^*_t)}_{\geq\, 0} + \underbrace{(V^*_t - V^{\pi_t}_t)}_{\text{true regret}} + \underbrace{(V^{\pi_t}_t - \Vlow_t)}_{\geq\, 0} \;\;\geq\;\; V^*_t - V^{\pi_t}_t.
\]
    
\end{proposition}


Thus, driving $\rho^\chi_t \to 0$ also causes the true regret to vanish, making this definition a meaningful notion of regret.  
Additionally, these quantities are estimated exclusively from the agent's past stream of experience, without reference to external quantities from the environment.

We view these two assumptions as the minimal conditions necessary for $\rho^\chi_t$ to be useful. If either fails to hold, then $\rho^\chi_t$ may not bound the true regret and give an invalid assessment. 
However, these assumptions are not sufficient to guarantee $\rho^\chi_t$ is useful. We may have that $\rho^\chi_t$ is an upper bound but never goes to zero, even if the agent finds an optimal policy. Consider the following example.
\vspace{-2mm}
\paragraph{Example: Vacuous upper bound and examiner.} Suppose the reward is bounded in $[0, R_{max}]$. Then, suppose we have an examiner $\chi_{bad}$ that outputs $\rho^{\chi_{bad}}_t = R_{max}  / (1-\gamma)$ at every step $t$. This quantity is an upper bound on the true value (and true regret) but does not depend on the data at all. Crucially, no matter how well the agent does, the bound will never decrease and $\rho_t$ is not a useful performance measure.


We seek an examiner that refine its estimates of $\rho^\chi_t$ as it receives more information. In other words, we want an examiner that \textit{learns}. This is a tricky requirement and  we discuss how to frame this theoretically in the following section.

The examiner is faced with a learning problem of its own. 
Given the stream of observations, rewards and actions, it must estimate $\Vhi$ and $\Vlow$ and produce the regret estimate $\rho^\chi_t$. 

Broadly-speaking, we desire to have an examiner $\chi$ that produces valid (in the sense of Prop. \ref{prop:valid}) estimates while keeping the bounds as tight as possible. 
Whenever the agent is learning effectively $\rho^\chi_t$ should decrease and, if the agent has an optimal policy, the examiner should output $\rho^\chi_t=0$.
Ideally, there would be a universal examiner $\chi^*$ applicable to any agent and any world while producing effective perceived regret estimates. 

Unfortunately, this is asking for too much. The situation is similar to generalization in supervised learning, where we cannot create a universal learning algorithm that generalizes well on any data distribution.
Similarly, we prove a no-free-lunch theorem showing that it is impossible to define a ``universal" examiner capable of accurately judging all agents in all worlds and discuss the implications.

Similar to no-free-lunch theorems in supervised learning, which tell us that over all data distributions, no model can outperform random guessing on the population loss, we find that it is impossible to produce valid non-vacuous bounds on all environments. 

where we cannot expect a learning algorithm to generalize well for all data distributions, we cannot expect the examiner to accurately assess any learner. 
The examiner must make some guess about the structure of the environment to account for possible futures and their associated rewards.  
This guess depends on the various modeling biases baked into the examiner. 
For example, the examiner may assume that the world is Markovian and produce its estimates in accordance with this assumption. This may be correct in certain environments and incorrect in others. 
The examiner's inductive bias also matters as the agent may enter previously unseen parts of the world and thus the examiner's generalization properties matter too.

Overall, we see that the examiner faces a learning problem at least as difficult as supervised learning, combining standard predictions on examples with a temporal and decision-making component. 
As such, it is expected that similar no-free-lunch theorems apply to the examiner. 
This means there are no ``universally" best examiners---we must make do with the examiners we believe are best fit for the environment at hand and cannot give general guarantees on their evaluation performance. 
Next, we discuss some choices of modeling biases we may endow the examiner with and reasonable criteria we could expect.

Since universal examiners are impossible, we need to make tradeoffs and choose properties we may want an examiner to have. To help us decide, we may prefer examiners that are known to work in specific types of environments of interest. 
We can assess an examiner by how it behaves in certain families of environments when run on known, optimal learning agents.

For example, in stochastic bandits and finite MDPs, there are known provably-optimal learning algorithms, achieving the best (true) expected regret possible. These agents will shrink the regret per step towards zero. 
A ``good" examiner, when run on these learning agents, should also reduce its perceived regret towards zero as the agent converges towards an optimal policy. 

We may be able to create more flexible examiners, capable of dealing with larger families of environments, by considering environments where provably-optimal learning agents exist. Drawing from the vast reinforcement learning theory literature, theorists have crafted increasingly general conditions where provably low regret is achievable. For example, tabular MDPs are a special case of linear MDPs \citep{jin2020provably}, which are in turn subsumed by low Bellman rank MDPs \citep{jiang2017contextual} or low Eluder dimension \citep{russo2013eluder}. These can be  further generalized with concepts such as the Bellman-Eluder dimension \citep{jin2021bellman}, bilinear classes \citep{du2021bilinear} and many alternative concepts \citep{ayoub2020model, sun2019model}. One of the most general family of environments where low regret is possible are those with low Decision-Estimation Coefficient \citep{foster2021statistical}. 
Whenever we have a provably optimal agent for some environment family, we can use this as a benchmark to test candidate examiners. In these cases, we can require that the examiner produces a perceived regret decreasing to zero at the similar rate as the true regret. 

It is important note that, in light of the no-free-lunch theorem, it will be impossible to expand the family of environments ad infinitum. The examiner will always be unreliable in some world. 
Certain examiners may also provide invalid perceived regret, which do not bound the true regret. In a general environment, we may not have any formal guarantee, similar to generalization in supervised learning. In practice, on a unknown environment, we  forgo any formal guarantees. 
% These are all modeling decisions that a use must make. 

Without a prior knowledge of the environment, we can mitigate the possibility of poor examiner by utilizing multiple examiners in parallel on a single agent's stream of experience, resulting in several performance estimates. These can aggregated or used a distribution of assessments. Similar to ensembles, we may want to choose a diverse set of examiners, with different modeling biases and learning mechanisms. 
We can also consider pretrained models (e.g. foundation models) that have assimilated a large corpus of data. This can lead to complex inductive biases within the examiner that could potentially help it produce more useful perceived regret values. 

In the next section, we illustrate some simple experiments where the appropriate choice of examiner for an agent and environment leads to meaningful perceived regret throughout the agent's learning process.

\textbf{Limitations.} The perceived regret is produced by an examiner, which is also a learning agent (that cannot influence the future). When deployed alongside the agent, there are no guarantees that its perceived regret is actually meaningful and it could be wildly incorrect. 
This may be an unavoidable price of seeking to evaluate agents in arbitrary worlds---the general applicability impacts the reliability of the assessment. 



\paragraph{Models evaluating other models.} The definition of perceived regret is similar in spirit to previously-used performance measures in the machine learning community. Generative models are one area where it has been common to assess the performance of one model with others. 
One example is the Inception score used as a performance metric to measure diversity and fidelity of a model's generated images by passing them through the Inception network. Another is CLIPScore in a similar context for test-to-image generation, utilizing the CLIP model as an evaluator. 
More recently, LLM-as-judge is a common framework used to automatically assess the outputs of a given LLM by feeding it to  multiple other LLMs.
LLMs have also been used to inspect their own outputs, critiquing and refining them to produce higher-quality answers \wes{add cite}.

\section{Conclusion}




\newpage






Reinforcement learning grades an agent from outside, against an optimum $V^{*}$ that a model, a prior, or a fixed comparator supplies \citep{lai1985asymptotically,jaksch2010ucrl2}. This is sound when the world is small enough to model. Continual reinforcement learning (CRL) assumes the opposite \citep{ring1994continual,abel2023definition}: under the big-world hypothesis the agent is far smaller than its environment, cannot represent or retain a fixed optimal solution, and never stops learning \citep{javed2025bigworld,lewandowski2025bigger,dohare2024plasticity}. Formally, two failures follow. At run time the agent has no estimator of $V^{*}$ that is measurable with respect to its own experience, and even given $V^{*}$ the quantity $V^{*}-f^{\pi_t}_t$ is the wrong objective, since a capacity-bounded agent should be compared to the best policy it can itself represent rather than to one it can never express \citep{simon1955behavioral,russell1995bounded,lieder2020resource,abel2024dogmas}.

Our position is that a continual agent's only honest measure of progress is one it computes from its own stream, and that to be trustworthy that measure must be produced by a module that out-learns the policy it grades. We make the measure explicit. Alongside the acting learner $\mathcal{L}$, an internal examiner $\mathcal{E}$ outputs an aspiration $\Vhi$ for the value it believes attainable and a guarantee $\Vlow$ for the value it is sure of, and the examiner regret is the gap $\rho_t=\Vhi-\Vlow\ge 0$. We make four formal claims. The self-report certifies nothing on its own (Proposition~\ref{prop:nothing}). A soundness condition makes $\rho_t$ an upper bound on the regret the agent can reduce (Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge}). In a coverable world this bound recovers the classical confidence-bound theory exactly (Proposition~\ref{prop:bandit}). And in a big world the bound cannot be driven to zero by any procedure, because the missing comparator is non-identifiable from the agent's stream (Lemma~\ref{lem:indist}, Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree}). The consequence is a design principle: the examiner must out-learn the learner, since estimating $\Vhi$ requires predicting the value of policies never executed. We claim no new rate; a bandit runs as the worked example throughout.

\section{Setting and Definitions}
\label{sec:defs}

We work over a single, never-resetting stream under an unknown law $\mu$, with no Markov, episodic, or stationarity assumption. Let $\Filt=\sigma(h_t)$ be the filtration generated by the observed history. We collect the standing assumptions.

\begin{assumption}
\label{ass:all}
\textnormal{(A1)} rewards satisfy $r_s\in[0,\Rmax]$. \textnormal{(A2)} value uses a fixed $H$-step look-ahead. \textnormal{(A3)} the learner is representable in $K_L$ bits, with policy class $\PiK$, $|\PiK|\le 2^{K_L}$. \textnormal{(A4)} the examiner carries an inductive bias, a model class $\ModE$ of laws it deems possible. \textnormal{(A5)} all guarantees hold with probability at least $1-\alpha$ over the stream. \textnormal{(A6)} the learner's policy $\pi_t$ and the examiner's outputs $\Vhi,\Vlow$ are $\Filt$-measurable; the agent acts on its stream alone, at any computational budget.
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}[Stream, policy, value]
\label{def:basic}
A history is $h_t=(o_0,r_0,a_1,\dots,a_t,o_t,r_t)$. A policy $\pi$ maps histories to action distributions, with value $f^{\pi}_t=\Ex_{\mu,\pi}\!\big[\sum_{s=t+1}^{t+H} r_s \mid h_t\big]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Within-capacity optimum and structural gap]
\label{def:capcomp}
The within-capacity optimum is $f^{K_L}_t=\sup_{\pi\in\PiK} f^{\pi}_t$, and the structural gap to the global optimum $f^{*}_t=\sup_{\pi}f^\pi_t$ is $\Dstruct_t=f^{*}_t-f^{K_L}_t\ge 0$. We compare the agent to $f^{K_L}_t$ and keep $\Dstruct_t$ explicit as the irreducible price of bounded capacity.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Learner and examiner]
\label{def:agent}
An agent is a pair $(\mathcal{L},\mathcal{E})$. The learner $\mathcal{L}$ emits $\pi_t\in\PiK$. The examiner $\mathcal{E}$ is an $\Filt$-measurable predictive model outputting $\Vhi\ge\Vlow$. Since $f^{K_L}_t$ depends on policies the learner may not run, estimating $\Vhi$ requires predicting the value of unexecuted behaviour, in the sense of general value functions \citep{sutton2011horde,modayil2014nexting} and off-policy prediction \citep{precup2001offpolicy,suttonprecupsingh1999}, with $\ModE$ the bias enabling generalization beyond the support of the data \citep{osband2013psrl}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Examiner regret]
\label{def:exreg}
$\rho_t=\Vhi-\Vlow\ge 0$, with $\Rex_T=\sum_{t=1}^T\rho_t$. As an $\Filt$-measurable functional, $\rho_t$ never refers to $\mu$, $f^{K_L}_t$, or $f^{*}_t$.
\end{definition}

\section{Where the External Comparator Fails}
\label{sec:fails}

The case for an internal measure is that the external comparator is, in the CRL regime, either undefined or non-estimable. Three cases make this precise. First, when the world's behaviour set grows without bound, the global optimum $f^{*}_t=\sup_{\pi}f^{\pi}_t$ is a moving and possibly unattained target, so static regret against a single $V^{*}$ has no fixed referent; our comparator $f^{K_L}_t$ stays well defined precisely because $\PiK$ is capacity-bounded with $|\PiK|\le 2^{K_L}$, which is one reason to prefer it. Second, even for a fixed action set, $f^{K_L}_t$ can depend on $\mu$ through regions the agent need never observe, so it need not be $\Filt$-measurable and may admit no estimator from the stream; we make this quantitative in Lemma~\ref{lem:indist}. Third, under non-stationarity the comparator $f^{K_L}_t$ varies in $t$, so a fixed-comparator regret can be negative or vacuous, and dynamic regret \citep{wei2021master,morrill2021hindsight} reinstates a moving optimum that is again non-estimable in a big world. In all three the obstruction is that the comparator lies outside $\Filt$. The examiner regret of Definition~\ref{def:exreg} is, by construction, an $\Filt$-measurable object; the rest of the paper asks what it is worth.

\section{A Self-Report Carries No Guarantee}
\label{sec:nothing}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:nothing}
There exists an examiner with $\rho_t\equiv 0$ for every learner and every $\mu$. Consequently, two agents with identical self-report $\rho_t\equiv 0$ can satisfy $f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t=0$ for all $t$ in one case and $f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t\ge c>0$ for all $t$ in the other.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Take $\Vhi=\Vlow=0$, which is $\Filt$-measurable and gives $\rho_t\equiv 0$ independent of $(\mathcal{L},\mu)$. Pair it with a learner playing a within-capacity optimum, and with one playing a policy of value $f^{K_L}_t-c$ at each $t$; both report $\rho_t\equiv0$. \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

Hence no guarantee can be read from $\rho_t$ alone. Trust requires a condition relating $\Vhi,\Vlow$ to value.

\section{Soundness Turns the Self-Report into a Certificate}
\label{sec:sound}

\begin{definition}[Soundness]
\label{def:sound}
The examiner is sound with slack $(\delta_t)$ if, with probability at least $1-\alpha$, for all $t$,
\[
\Vlow\le f^{\pi_t}_t \quad\text{(floor)} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \Vhi\ge f^{K_L}_t-\delta_t \quad\text{(ceiling)} .
\]
It is consistent if $\tfrac1T\sum_{t\le T}\delta_t\to 0$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Certificate]
\label{lem:bridge}
If the examiner is sound with slack $(\delta_t)$ then, with probability at least $1-\alpha$,
\[
\sum_{t=1}^T\big(f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t\big)\;\le\;\Rex_T+\sum_{t=1}^T\delta_t,
\qquad
\sum_{t=1}^T\big(f^{*}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t\big)\;\le\;\Rex_T+\sum_{t=1}^T\delta_t+\sum_{t=1}^T\Dstruct_t .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
On the event of Definition~\ref{def:sound}, decompose $f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t=(f^{K_L}_t-\Vhi)+(\Vhi-\Vlow)+(\Vlow-f^{\pi_t}_t)$. The first summand is at most $\delta_t$ by the ceiling, the third is at most $0$ by the floor, and the middle is $\rho_t$. Summing over $t$ gives the first inequality; adding $\sum_t\Dstruct_t=\sum_t(f^{*}_t-f^{K_L}_t)$ gives the second. \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

The identity is the optimism and performance-difference decomposition \citep{kakade2002cpi,jaksch2010ucrl2}; the content is that under soundness the $\Filt$-measurable $\Rex_T$ upper-bounds a quantity, $\sum_t(f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t)$, that is not itself $\Filt$-measurable. Meeting the ceiling demands strictly more than acting: by Definition~\ref{def:sound} the examiner must lower-bound $f^{K_L}_t$, a supremum over $\PiK$, whereas the learner need only realize one $\pi_t\in\PiK$. This is the formal sense in which the examiner must out-learn the learner.

\section{What the Bound Recovers}
\label{sec:recover}

In the coverable case the construction is exactly the classical confidence-bound theory under a new name. Let $|\mathcal{S}|=1$, $H=1$ (a bandit) with $N$ arms of mean $\mu_i$, $\mu^{\star}=\max_i\mu_i$, so $f^{\pi}_t=\mu_i$ for ``pull $i$'' and $f^{K_L}_t=\mu^{\star}$. Let $n_i(t)$ be the pull count, $\hat\mu_i$ the empirical mean, and $c_i(t)=\sqrt{2\ln t/n_i(t)}$.

\begin{proposition}[Recovery of UCB and pseudo-regret]
\label{prop:bandit}
The examiner $\Vhi=\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)$, $\Vlow=\hat\mu_{a_t}-c_{a_t}$ satisfies the following. \textnormal{(i)} It is sound with slack $\delta_t\to 0$ with high probability. \textnormal{(ii)} $\rho_t=2c_{a_t}$ is twice the played arm's confidence width, and $\sum_t(f^{K_L}_t-f^{\pi_t}_t)=\sum_t(\mu^{\star}-\mu_{a_t})$ is the classical pseudo-regret. \textnormal{(iii)} $\Rex_T=O(\sqrt{NT\log T})$, matching the UCB1 rate \citep{auer2002ucb}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textnormal{(i)} By Hoeffding, $\Pr(|\hat\mu_i-\mu_i|>c_i)\le 2e^{-2n_i c_i^2}=2t^{-4}$ for each arm at each round; summing over arms and rounds, the bounds $\hat\mu_i+c_i\ge\mu_i$ for all $i$ and $\hat\mu_{a_t}-c_{a_t}\le\mu_{a_t}$ hold for all $t\le T$ with high probability under the standard UCB1 constants \citep{auer2002ucb}. On that event $\Vhi=\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)\ge\mu^{\star}$ (ceiling, $\delta_t=0$) and $\Vlow\le\mu_{a_t}=f^{\pi_t}_t$ (floor). \textnormal{(ii)} Since $a_t=\arg\max_i(\hat\mu_i+c_i)$, the maximizing index is the played arm's, so $\Vhi=\hat\mu_{a_t}+c_{a_t}$ and $\rho_t=\Vhi-\Vlow=2c_{a_t}$; the pseudo-regret identity is immediate from $f^{\pi}_t=\mu_i$ and $f^{K_L}_t=\mu^{\star}$. \textnormal{(iii)} Using $\sum_{k=1}^{n_i}k^{-1/2}\le 2\sqrt{n_i}$ and $\ln t\le\ln T$, $\Rex_T=2\sum_t c_{a_t}\le 2\sqrt{2\ln T}\sum_i\sum_{k=1}^{n_i}k^{-1/2}\le 4\sqrt{2\ln T}\sum_i\sqrt{n_i}\le 4\sqrt{2\ln T}\,\sqrt{N\textstyle\sum_i n_i}=4\sqrt{2NT\ln T}$ by Cauchy--Schwarz, since $\sum_i n_i=T$. \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

Thus $\rho_t$ is not exotic: it is the UCB/LCB pair, and Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} reads off the UCB1 guarantee. When the agent covers its arms, $\delta_t\to 0$ and $\rho_t\to 0$, so the certificate is tight and vanishing, and the agent bounds its distance from $\mu^{\star}$ without ever computing $\mu^{\star}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:bandit}A).

\section{The Price: No Free Self-Evaluation in a Big World}
\label{sec:nofree}

Coverage is what a big world denies. The obstruction is information, not computation, and it is exactly non-identifiability.

\begin{lemma}[Indistinguishability and its price]
\label{lem:indist}
Let $\mu,\mu'$ satisfy $\Pr_\mu=\Pr_{\mu'}$ on $\Filt$. Then under \textnormal{(A6)} every agent output has the same law under $\mu$ and $\mu'$, and for any world functional $\theta$ and any $\Filt$-measurable estimator $\hat\theta$,
\[
\max_{\nu\in\{\mu,\mu'\}}\Ex_\nu\big|\hat\theta-\theta(\nu)\big|\;\ge\;\tfrac12\,\big|\theta(\mu)-\theta(\mu')\big|.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
An $\Filt$-measurable map is a function of $h_t$, whose law is identical under $\mu,\mu'$; hence its push-forward law is identical \textbf{[proved]}. The inequality is Le Cam's two-point bound with $\TV(\Pr_\mu,\Pr_{\mu'})=0$ on $\Filt$ \citep{tsybakov2009} \textbf{[cited]}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[No free self-evaluation]
\label{thm:nofree}
Consider the family of $N$-armed bandits in which one arm pays $\tfrac12+\epsilon$ and the rest pay $\tfrac12$, with the special arm unknown, run for horizon $T$. On a worst-case member, with probability at least $1-\alpha$, a sound examiner has
\[
\rho_t\;\ge\;\epsilon-\delta_t \ \text{ for all } t,\qquad \Rex_T\;\ge\;\epsilon T-\sum_{t\le T}\delta_t,
\]
unless it closes (i) a coverage gap, $T\ge N$ so that every arm is pulled, or (ii) a bias gap, $\ModE$ distinguishes the special arm from an ordinary one.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Coverage gap.} If $T<N$ the learner pulls at most $T<N$ distinct arms, so some arm $i^{*}$ is unpulled. Let $\mu_0$ be all-$\tfrac12$ and $\mu_{i^{*}}$ make $i^{*}$ special; the two differ only at $i^{*}$, so $\Pr_{\mu_0}=\Pr_{\mu_{i^{*}}}$ on $\Filt$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:indist} with $\theta=f^{K_L}$, the examiner emits the same $(\Vhi,\Vlow)$ in both, while $f^{K_L}$ differs by $\epsilon$. Under $\mu_{i^{*}}$ the played value is $\tfrac12$, so the floor forces $\Vlow\le\tfrac12$ and the ceiling forces $\Vhi\ge(\tfrac12+\epsilon)-\delta_t$; hence $\rho_t\ge\epsilon-\delta_t$, and the true regret is $\epsilon T$.
\emph{Bias gap.} If instead every arm is pulled but $\ModE$ maps the special-arm world and an ordinary world to a common forecast, an examiner trusting $\ModE$ sets $\Vhi\approx\tfrac12<(\tfrac12+\epsilon)-\delta_t$ and is unsound where that arm is special; restoring the ceiling forces a hedge of size $\epsilon$, i.e.\ $\rho_t\ge\epsilon-\delta_t$. \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

Two reading rules follow. First, the bound holds over all $\Filt$-measurable procedures, so no computational budget escapes it: extra compute tightens estimates only within what $\Filt$ and $\ModE$ determine. Second, a consistent examiner ($\delta_t\to 0$) reporting $\rho_t\to 0$ in a big world is, by Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree}, relying on an unverified $\ModE$, so a near-zero $\rho_t$ is unearned confidence, not completed learning. The impossibility direction is not novel: gaps (i) and (ii) are the explore-or-assume dichotomy and the realizability requirement \citep{kearns2002e3,brafman2002rmax,azar2017minimax,lai1985asymptotically}. We use it to price the position.

Figure~\ref{fig:bandit} contrasts the regimes against the best the external view can compute, namely the plug-in estimate $\max_i\hat\mu_i-\hat\mu_{a_t}$ of static regret (and, with a sliding window, of dynamic regret \citep{wei2021master}). With coverage (panel A) examiner regret vanishes with the true regret. Without it (panel B), and under non-stationarity (panel C, where $f^{K_L}_t$ moves and consistency cannot hold cheaply), the plug-in estimate underestimates the true regret, declaring the agent done, while examiner regret remains a valid upper bound.

\section{Building an Examiner that Out-Learns the Learner}
\label{sec:practical}

The principle is constructive: $\Vhi$ is any optimistic estimate, a UCB index \citep{auer2002ucb}, optimistic value iteration \citep{jaksch2010ucrl2}, an ensemble upper quantile \citep{osband2016bootstrapped}, or a posterior upper quantile \citep{osband2013psrl}, and $\Vlow$ any pessimistic one \citep{kumar2020cql,jin2021pessimism}. The remaining question is how an $\Filt$-measurable examiner can lower-bound $f^{K_L}_t=\sup_{\pi\in\PiK}f^\pi_t$ when the learner realizes only one $\pi_t$. Three mechanisms answer it, none of which is merely a better prior. \emph{Off-policy estimation} of the value of a family of policies the learner never runs, through off-policy temporal-difference learning and general value functions \citep{sutton2011horde,precup2001offpolicy,modayil2014nexting}, is exactly what estimating $\Vhi$ requires. \emph{Asymmetric capacity and a faster timescale} let the examiner track ahead of the policy it grades \citep{pinto2018asymmetric,konda2000actorcritic,borkar1997twotimescale}. And a \emph{calibration target} \citep{foster1998calibration} makes the slack $\delta_t$ shrink on the covered region, rendering ``is the examiner learning?'' operational. By Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} none of the three manufactures information, so the lead is real but capped by coverage; the bound becomes a design rule, namely to separate the critic from the actor and grant it its own budget $K_E$, off-policy targets, and a calibration loss, with the split $K=K_L+K_E$ the open problem the position opens. For a deployed agent that cannot be graded against an optimum, $\rho_t$ is then an online, $\Filt$-measurable read of self-assessed room to improve, and Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} is the operator's warning that a small $\rho_t$ without coverage is unearned.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

The content lives in Definition~\ref{def:sound}, not in $\rho_t$, and soundness is extrinsic, requiring $\ModE$ and hence a prior. Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} is a decomposition, the optimism gap relabeled, not a new rate. Proposition~\ref{prop:bandit} is tight only in the coverable bandit; soundness of a biased, capacity-bounded examiner under non-stationarity, and the under-capacity case $\Dstruct_t>0$, are not established \textbf{[GAP]}. Figure~\ref{fig:bandit} is an illustration on a synthetic bandit, not a general guarantee. Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} and Lemma~\ref{lem:indist} are worst-case two-point statements and not themselves novel, and we do not characterize which $\ModE$ avoid the bias gap on which families. The out-learning principle is a design argument, not a theorem that an off-policy examiner attains soundness faster than the learner attains optimality \textbf{[GAP]}. We propose one coupling, $\rho_t$ as intrinsic reward, but do not analyze the joint dynamics.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Foundations.} \citet{ring1994continual,abel2023definition,abel2024dogmas,abel2020bounded} develop CRL and the agent-centric view; bounded optimality and resource-rational analysis \citep{simon1955behavioral,russell1995bounded,lewis2014computational,lieder2020resource} motivate the comparator $f^{K_L}$ and the reward hypothesis \citep{silver2021reward} the goal it tracks, while the big-world hypothesis \citep{javed2025bigworld,lewandowski2025bigger,dohare2024plasticity} drives the removal of the external yardstick. \textbf{Machinery.} General value functions and off-policy prediction \citep{sutton2011horde,modayil2014nexting,suttonprecupsingh1999,precup2001offpolicy} and asymmetric and two-timescale actor critic \citep{pinto2018asymmetric,konda2000actorcritic,borkar1997twotimescale} let the examiner learn about behaviours it does not enact; optimistic and pessimistic value learning \citep{kearns2002e3,brafman2002rmax,jaksch2010ucrl2,auer2002ucb,strehl2009pacmdp,azar2017minimax,kakade2002cpi,kumar2020cql,jin2021pessimism,osband2016bootstrapped} supply its two numbers, and the two-point method \citep{lai1985asymptotically,tsybakov2009} its price. \textbf{Closest.} Bayesian and capacity-limited RL \citep{osband2013psrl,russo2017ids,lu2021bitbybit,arumugam2024capacity} and model-value inconsistency \citep{filos2022modelvalue} read a value gap as uncertainty; self-assessment and calibration \citep{schmidhuber2010formal,oudeyer2007intrinsic,bellemare2016count,pathak2017curiosity,burda2019rnd,foster1998calibration,li2011kwik,morrill2021hindsight} bear on honest progress. In each, the evaluator is external or fused with the policy, whereas we make it an explicit, continually-learning, $\Filt$-measurable, capacity-bounded module and ask what its self-report certifies.
