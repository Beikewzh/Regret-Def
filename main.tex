\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A central premise of continual reinforcement learning (CRL) is that the agent is far smaller than its world: under the \emph{big-world hypothesis}, no agent can represent, learn, or retain a fixed optimal solution, so the best agents never stop learning \citep{abel2023definition,javed2025bigworld,lewandowski2025bigger}. This premise quietly breaks the tool we use to certify that an agent is learning. Regret measures the agent against an optimum $V^{*}$ obtained from a model, a prior, or a comparator class supplied from outside \citep{lai1985asymptotically,jaksch2010ucrl2}. In an open-ended world the agent has no such outside source at run time; and even a known $V^{*}$ would be the wrong yardstick, because a bounded agent cannot represent the optimal policy and should be judged against the best use of its own resources \citep{russell1995bounded,lewis2014computational,lieder2020resource}.

\paragraph{Position.}
We adopt the agent's-eye view of evaluation. If a continual agent is to know whether it is still learning, it must compute that judgment itself, from its own stream of experience, within its own finite capacity. We make this judgment an explicit object: alongside the acting \emph{learner}, an internal \emph{examiner} forecasts an \emph{aspiration} for the best value it believes attainable and a \emph{guarantee} for the value it is sure it achieves, and the gap between them, the \emph{examiner regret} $\rho_t$, is the agent's self-assessed room to improve. Our thesis is that this self-assessment is meaningful only at a price, and that the price is paid in a currency CRL has not named: \emph{the examiner must be the more strongly biased module}. To judge whether it could have done better, the agent must reason about choices it never tried, which requires a model of the world richer than the policy it acts with. We show this bias is both necessary and double-edged, and that in a big world honest self-assessment is never free.

\paragraph{What we do.} We define examiner regret and observe that, on its own, it certifies nothing: an agent can simply declare it to be zero (Proposition~\ref{prop:nothing}). We isolate the missing ingredient as a \emph{soundness} condition and show it makes $\rho_t$ a certificate of the regret a bounded agent can actually reduce (Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge}), recovering the classical bandit rate when the agent can cover its world. We then prove the central claim: a sound examiner with a vanishing self-report requires either complete coverage of the world or a correct inductive bias, and neither is available for free in a big world (Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree}). We close with how $\rho_t$ can be estimated and used in practice, and with an explicit account of the limitations. We claim no new algorithm or rate; the contribution is the position, a definition that makes it precise, and a lower bound that gives it teeth.

\section{A Learner That Acts and an Examiner That Judges}
\label{sec:defs}

\paragraph{Setting.} An agent interacts with an unknown environment over a single, never-resetting stream of experience; we assume no Markov, episodic, or stationarity structure. Rewards lie in $[0,\Rmax]$, and we measure value over an $H$-step look-ahead. The agent is capacity-bounded: it represents policies and beliefs within a fixed budget of bits. We seek a definition of a learning measure the agent can compute from its own state, the condition under which it is trustworthy, and the limit beyond which it cannot be.

\begin{definition}[Stream, policy, value]
\label{def:basic}
A \emph{history} is $h_t = (o_0,r_0,a_1,\dots,a_t,o_t,r_t)$, with observations $o_s$, rewards $r_s \in [0,\Rmax]$, actions $a_s$; the underlying law is $\mu$. A \emph{policy} $\pi$ maps histories to action distributions, with value $f^{\pi}_t = \Ex_{\mu,\pi}\!\big[\sum_{s=t+1}^{t+H} r_s \mid h_t\big]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[What the learner could have done]
\label{def:capcomp}
The learner represents policies within $K_L$ bits; write $\PiK$ for this representable set. The \emph{within-capacity optimum} is $f^{K_L}_t = \sup_{\pi \in \PiK} f^{\pi}_t$, and the \emph{structural gap} to the global optimum is $\Dstruct_t = f^{*}_t - f^{K_L}_t \ge 0$. We compare the agent to $f^{K_L}_t$, the best it could itself express, and keep $\Dstruct_t$ visible as the irreducible price of being small.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Learner and examiner]
\label{def:agent}
An agent is a pair $(\mathcal{L},\mathcal{E})$. The \emph{learner} $\mathcal{L}$ emits the policy $\pi_t \in \PiK$ it acts with. The \emph{examiner} $\mathcal{E}$ carries an \emph{inductive bias}, a model class $\ModE$ of environments it considers possible, and from the history outputs an \emph{aspiration} $\Vhi$ and a \emph{guarantee} $\Vlow$, with $\Vhi \ge \Vlow$. The examiner's role is to judge, not to act: setting $\Vhi$ requires reasoning about the value of policies the learner did not run, which is why $\ModE$ must be richer than a single policy.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Examiner regret]
\label{def:exreg}
$\rho_t = \Vhi - \Vlow \ge 0$, with $\Rex_T = \sum_{t=1}^T \rho_t$. It depends only on the history and the examiner; it never refers to $\mu$ or to any optimum.
\end{definition}

\section{A Self-Report Carries No Guarantee}
\label{sec:nothing}

The definition is cheap on purpose, and the first thing to say is what that cheapness costs.

\begin{proposition}[Examiner regret says nothing on its own]
\label{prop:nothing}
There is an examiner that reports $\rho_t \equiv 0$ for every learner and every world. Hence two agents with the identical self-report $\rho_t \equiv 0$ can differ arbitrarily in actual performance: one optimal within its capacity, the other suboptimal by a constant at every step.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let the examiner always output $\Vhi = \Vlow = 0$, so $\rho_t \equiv 0$ regardless of behaviour or environment. Pair it once with a learner that plays a within-capacity optimum, and once with a learner that plays a policy worth a constant less at each step. Both report $\rho_t \equiv 0$.
\end{proof}

So examiner regret guarantees nothing by itself; trust must come from a condition tying the examiner's two numbers to real value. That condition is the subject of the paper, and it is where the examiner's bias enters.

\section{Soundness, and Why the Examiner Must Be Biased}
\label{sec:sound}

\begin{definition}[Soundness]
\label{def:sound}
The examiner is \emph{sound} with slack $(\delta_t)$ if, with high probability, for all $t$: the guarantee is a true floor, $\Vlow \le f^{\pi_t}_t$, and the aspiration is a true ceiling for the within-capacity optimum, $\Vhi \ge f^{K_L}_t - \delta_t$. It is \emph{consistent} if the average slack vanishes, $\frac1T\sum_{t\le T}\delta_t \to 0$.
\end{definition}

Soundness rules out the empty report of Proposition~\ref{prop:nothing}: the all-zero examiner violates the ceiling condition whenever the agent could do better than zero. The substantive question is how an examiner can \emph{meet} the ceiling condition, since it must set $\Vhi$ above the value of policies the learner may never have run. It has only two ways to do so. It can rely on \emph{coverage}, having actually seen those policies tried; or it can \emph{extrapolate} from its model class $\ModE$, inferring their value from structure it assumes. The second is an inductive bias, and it is what lets a small examiner judge a large space cheaply. This is the precise sense in which the examiner must be more strongly biased than the learner: acting needs only a policy, but judging counterfactual policies needs a world model.

\begin{lemma}[Soundness turns the self-report into a certificate]
\label{lem:bridge}
If the examiner is sound with slack $(\delta_t)$, then with high probability the agent's regret against its within-capacity optimum obeys
\[
\sum_{t=1}^T \big(f^{K_L}_t - f^{\pi_t}_t\big) \;\le\; \Rex_T + \sum_{t=1}^T \delta_t,
\]
and the regret against the global optimum adds only the irreducible $\sum_t \Dstruct_t$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For each $t$, write $f^{K_L}_t - f^{\pi_t}_t = (f^{K_L}_t - \Vhi) + (\Vhi - \Vlow) + (\Vlow - f^{\pi_t}_t)$. By soundness the first term is at most $\delta_t$ and the third is at most $0$, while the middle term is $\rho_t$; summing gives the first inequality. Adding $\sum_t \Dstruct_t = \sum_t(f^{*}_t - f^{K_L}_t)$ gives the global statement.
\end{proof}

This is a decomposition, not a new rate: the identity is the optimism gap familiar from confidence-bound analyses \citep{jaksch2010ucrl2}. Its point is interpretive. Under soundness, the cheap self-report becomes an upper bound on the regret a bounded agent can actually drive down, with the unremovable structural cost shown rather than hidden.

\paragraph{When the agent can cover its world, the certificate is tight.}
In a $K$-armed bandit whose arms the learner can all represent and try, the examiner needs no bias at all: the aspiration is the largest upper confidence bound and the guarantee the lower confidence bound of the played arm. By Hoeffding's inequality these are sound with vanishing slack, and the standard UCB1 analysis gives $\Rex_T = O(\sqrt{KT\log T})$ \citep{auer2002ucb}, so by Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} the within-capacity regret matches the classical rate. \textbf{[cited]} Coverage, not cleverness, is what makes the slack vanish here, and coverage is exactly what a big world denies.

\section{No Free Self-Evaluation in a Big World}
\label{sec:nofree}

We now show the central claim. When the world is larger than the agent can cover, a sound examiner can keep its self-report small only by trusting a bias, and that bias cannot be verified, so it can be wrong. Either way the agent pays.

\begin{theorem}[No free self-evaluation]
\label{thm:nofree}
Consider a family of $N$-armed bandits in which exactly one ``special'' arm pays $\tfrac12+\epsilon$ and the rest pay $\tfrac12$; which arm is special is unknown. Run any agent for a horizon $T$. Then on a worst-case member of the family, with high probability,
\[
\rho_t \;\ge\; \epsilon - \delta_t \quad\text{for all } t, \qquad\text{so}\qquad \Rex_T \;\ge\; \epsilon T - \sum_{t\le T}\delta_t,
\]
unless the examiner removes both of the following gaps:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] \emph{a coverage gap}: if $T < N$ the learner leaves some arm untried, and the special arm can be placed there;
\item[(ii)] \emph{a bias gap}: if the examiner's model class $\ModE$ cannot tell the special arm apart from an ordinary one, observing the others does not reveal it.
\end{itemize}
Removing (i) requires trying every arm ($T \ge N$); removing (ii) requires $\ModE$ to contain the truth, which the agent cannot check against arms it has not resolved. Consequently a consistent examiner ($\delta_t \to 0$) that reports $\rho_t \to 0$ in a big world must be relying on an unverified bias, and if that bias is wrong it is not sound.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Coverage gap.} Let $\mu_0$ be the all-$\tfrac12$ bandit and run the agent in it. In $T$ steps it pulls at most $T$ distinct arms, so if $T < N$ some arm $i^{*}$ is never pulled. Let $\mu_{i^{*}}$ make arm $i^{*}$ special. Since the two worlds differ only at $i^{*}$, which is never pulled, the histories coincide and the examiner outputs the same numbers in both. Under $\mu_{i^{*}}$ the played policy is worth $\tfrac12$, so soundness forces the guarantee $\Vlow \le \tfrac12$ and the aspiration $\Vhi \ge (\tfrac12+\epsilon) - \delta_t$; hence $\rho_t \ge \epsilon - \delta_t$, and summing gives the bound. The agent also never finds the special arm, so its true regret is $\epsilon T$.

\emph{Bias gap.} Suppose instead the learner does try every arm, but the examiner's class $\ModE$ asserts all arms share a common mean (a natural exchangeability bias). Having seen the ordinary arms average $\tfrac12$, an examiner that trusts $\ModE$ predicts the special arm at $\tfrac12$ as well, so it sets $\Vhi \approx \tfrac12 < (\tfrac12+\epsilon) - \delta_t$ and is \emph{unsound} on the world where that arm is special. To restore soundness it must widen the aspiration to cover the possibility, which returns it to a hedge of size $\epsilon$, i.e.\ $\rho_t \ge \epsilon - \delta_t$ again.

In both cases a sound examiner cannot also have $\rho_t < \epsilon - \delta_t$; escaping requires closing the coverage gap (try everything) or the bias gap (a correct, and here unverifiable, model class). \textbf{[proved]}
\end{proof}

The reading is the position made precise. Self-evaluation in a big world is bought with one of two coins: \emph{exploration}, which buys coverage but is impossible when the world outruns the horizon, or \emph{prior knowledge}, which buys extrapolation but only if it happens to be right and which the agent cannot audit on what it has not seen. The examiner's power is exactly the bias it carries; that bias is necessary to judge a large world from limited experience, and it is double-edged, because a confident wrong bias produces a small but false self-report. A near-zero $\rho_t$ in a genuinely big world should therefore be read as unearned confidence, not as completed learning. This also makes the often-questioned consistency assumption honest: in a big world it cannot hold cheaply, and the theorem says exactly why.

\paragraph{The budgeting question.} The favourable bandit and the worst-case family are the two sides of one coin: how much of the world the agent can resolve, by exploring or by correctly assuming. The total budget $K = K_L + K_E$ governs both, since bits given to the examiner buy a richer, more reliable bias while bits given to the learner buy reach and coverage. Characterizing the split that best trades exploration against bias, and doing so under genuine non-stationarity, is the central problem the position opens.

\section{Estimating and Using Examiner Regret}
\label{sec:practical}

The construction is directly estimable, which is what makes the position usable. \textbf{[heuristic]} An aspiration $\Vhi$ can be read off an optimistic value estimate, the upper head of a value-function ensemble or a UCB-style critic, and a guarantee $\Vlow$ off a pessimistic one, the lower head or a conservative critic \citep{lu2021bitbybit,arumugam2024capacity}; their gap is $\rho_t$, computed online with no access to $V^{*}$, and the ensemble's prior is the examiner's bias. Three uses follow. As an \emph{exploration signal}, acting to reduce $\rho_t$ steers the agent toward states where its optimism and pessimism disagree, a value-unit relative of learning-progress drives \citep{schmidhuber2010formal,oudeyer2007intrinsic}. As a \emph{comparison metric}, running different algorithms on one continual stream and comparing their $\rho_t$ trajectories estimates which is closer to its own capacity-constrained optimum without needing $V^{*}$. And because soundness is the calibration of the ensemble on what has been seen, a practitioner can \emph{audit} a low $\rho_t$, while Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} warns that on the unseen no audit is possible. We do not run these experiments; we note the path because it turns the position into a tool.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\textbf{(L1)} The content lives in soundness, not in $\rho_t$: Proposition~\ref{prop:nothing} is explicit that the self-report alone certifies nothing, and soundness is extrinsic, requiring the examiner to carry a model class and hence a prior. \textbf{(L2)} Lemma~\ref{lem:bridge} is a decomposition, the optimism gap relabeled, not a new rate; its value is conceptual. \textbf{(L3)} Our tight, positive result is shown only for the coverable bandit; soundness of a biased, capacity-bounded examiner under genuine non-stationarity, and the under-capacity case $\Dstruct_t > 0$, are not established here. \textbf{[GAP]} \textbf{(L4)} Theorem~\ref{thm:nofree} is a worst-case statement over a family, in the manner of two-point lower bounds \citep{lai1985asymptotically}; it does not bound a fixed environment, and the bias gap is shown for one natural exchangeability bias rather than for every $\ModE$. \textbf{(L5)} The learner and examiner are separate maps with no feedback channel, so the budgeting between them is conceptual, with no mechanism in the present formalism. \textbf{(L6)} The capacity model counts representable policies and ignores computation. \textbf{(L7)} Relative to capacity-limited Bayesian RL \citep{arumugam2024capacity}, which already uses within-capacity targets, the novelty is the explicit examiner, the self-report-certifies-nothing thesis, and the coverage-or-bias lower bound, not a new upper bound. \textbf{(L8)} There are no experiments; the recipe of Section~\ref{sec:practical} is untested.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Foundations and bounded rationality.} \citet{abel2023definition,abel2024dogmas,abel2020bounded} define and develop CRL and the bounded-rationality view; bounded optimality \citep{russell1995bounded}, computational rationality \citep{lewis2014computational}, and resource-rational analysis \citep{lieder2020resource} motivate judging a bounded agent against its own resources, our comparator $f^{K_L}$. \textbf{Big-world hypothesis.} \citet{javed2025bigworld,lewandowski2025bigger}. \textbf{Learning progress.} \citet{schmidhuber2010formal,oudeyer2007intrinsic} give self-assessed drives; $\rho_t$ is a value-unit relative with a soundness target. \textbf{Capacity-bounded and Bayes-adaptive RL.} \citet{lu2021bitbybit} cast capacity in bits; \citet{arumugam2024capacity} build capacity-bounded posteriors with within-capacity targets, the closest prior work; BAMDP and AIXI \citep{guez2012bamcp,lidayan2025bampf,hutter2005aixi} supply the model-class examiner and its idealized limit. \textbf{Regret, calibration, certification.} Confidence-bound and PAC analyses \citep{jaksch2010ucrl2,auer2002ucb,strehl2009pacmdp}, calibration \citep{foster1998calibration}, KWIK's ``knows what it knows'' \citep{li2011kwik}, non-stationary regret \citep{wei2021master}, and hindsight rationality \citep{morrill2021hindsight} all bear on honest self-assessment; across them the evaluator is external or fused with the policy, whereas we make it an explicit, biased, capacity-bounded module and ask what its self-report is worth.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Continual reinforcement learning has no examiner standing outside the agent, so the regret that matters is the one a bounded internal examiner can certify. We have argued, and made precise, that such self-evaluation is meaningful only under a soundness condition, that meeting it forces the examiner to be the more strongly biased module because judging counterfactuals needs a world model, and that in a big world this is never free: a small, trustworthy self-report demands either coverage the horizon does not allow or a prior the agent cannot verify. The picture turns evaluation in CRL into a budgeting problem between acting and judging, gives a directly estimable learning-progress and comparison signal, and warns against trusting an agent that reports it has finished learning. We claim no new algorithm or rate; the contribution is the position, a definition that carries it, and a lower bound that shows the price is real.
